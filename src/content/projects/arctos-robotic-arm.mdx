## Overview
**Arctos 6-DOF Robotic Arm** is a fully 3D-printed, 6-degree-of-freedom robotic arm based on the open-source Arctos design. The arm is controlled via a custom Python orchestration layer, Arduino-based firmware written in C++, and Arctos Studio for graphical motion planning and inverse kinematics.

---

## 👨‍💻 Role
**Hardware & Software Engineer** — responsible for assembly, firmware development, and high-level control software.

---

## ❓ Problem
Off-the-shelf robotic arms are expensive and offer limited customizability for research and educational purposes. An open-source, 3D-printable alternative enables hands-on learning in robotics, kinematics, and embedded systems at low cost.

---

## 🎯 Goal
1. Assemble and calibrate a fully functional 6-DOF robotic arm from 3D-printed components.
2. Write and flash reliable firmware to the Arduino controllers for precise servo and stepper motor control.
3. Integrate Python-based high-level control to enable programmable motion sequences.

---

## ✨ Solution

### Hardware
- **Structure:** All structural components 3D-printed using PLA/PETG with joints designed for each degree of freedom.
- **Actuators:** Stepper motors at each joint driven by Arduino-compatible motor drivers.
- **Electronics:** Arduino board manages low-level PWM signals; components wired for clean power distribution.

### Software
- **Arduino Firmware (C++):** Handles real-time motor stepping, limit switches, and serial command parsing.
- **Python Control Layer:** Sends high-level motion commands over serial to the Arduino, enabling scripted sequences.
- **Arctos Studio:** Provides graphical inverse kinematics and joint angle planning, exporting G-code-style commands.

### Key Features
1. **6 Degrees of Freedom:** Full shoulder, elbow, wrist, and gripper range of motion.
2. **Inverse Kinematics:** Arctos Studio computes joint angles from target end-effector positions.
3. **Python Scripted Sequences:** Programmable pick-and-place routines via Python serial interface.

---

## ⚙️ Challenges and Learnings
1. **Mechanical Tolerance:** 3D-printed parts required iterative calibration to eliminate backlash in joints.
2. **Firmware Reliability:** Debouncing limit switches and managing stepper acceleration curves in C++ required careful tuning.
3. **Coordinate Frames:** Understanding the transformation between world coordinates and joint space was the steepest learning curve.

---

## ✨ Final Thoughts
1. **Embedded and software skills complement each other** — getting the arm working required equal parts hardware debugging and software design.
2. **Open-source robotics** is a powerful educational tool; the Arctos community provided invaluable documentation.
3. This project deepened my interest in combining embedded systems with higher-level AI/ML for autonomous manipulation.
