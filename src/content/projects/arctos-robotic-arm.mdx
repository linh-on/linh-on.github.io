## Overview

This project is a fully 3D-printed, 6-degree-of-freedom robotic arm based on the open-source Arctos design. The arm combines three layers of control: Arduino firmware written in C++ for real-time motor control, a custom Python layer for scripted motion sequences, and Arctos Studio for graphical motion planning and inverse kinematics.

## Problem

Off-the-shelf robotic arms are expensive and offer limited customizability for learning and experimentation. Building an open-source, 3D-printable alternative makes hands-on work in robotics, kinematics, and embedded systems accessible at a fraction of the cost, while exposing every layer of the stack instead of hiding it behind a vendor's software.

## My Role

I was the sole hardware and software engineer on this build. I assembled and calibrated the full mechanical structure, developed and flashed the Arduino firmware, and wrote the Python control layer that drives programmable motion sequences over serial.

## Key Features

- **Six degrees of freedom.** Full shoulder, elbow, wrist, and gripper range of motion, enabling complex poses and pick-and-place tasks.
- **Inverse kinematics.** Arctos Studio computes joint angles from target end-effector positions, so motion can be planned in world coordinates rather than raw joint angles.
- **Scripted motion sequences.** A Python serial interface sends high-level commands to the firmware, making programmable pick-and-place routines simple to write and repeat.
- **Fully 3D-printed structure.** Every structural component is printed in PLA/PETG, keeping the build inexpensive and easy to modify or repair.

## Tech Stack

- **Structure:** 3D-printed PLA/PETG components with joints designed for each degree of freedom
- **Actuators:** Stepper motors at each joint, driven by Arduino-compatible motor drivers
- **Firmware:** Arduino C++ handling real-time motor stepping, limit switches, and serial command parsing
- **Control:** Python layer sending high-level motion commands over serial
- **Motion planning:** Arctos Studio for graphical inverse kinematics and joint angle planning, exporting G-code-style commands

## Development Process

The build progressed from the ground up: printing and assembling the structural components, wiring the steppers and drivers with clean power distribution, then bringing up the firmware one joint at a time. Once each joint moved reliably with proper limit-switch handling, the Python serial layer was added for multi-joint scripted sequences, and finally Arctos Studio was integrated for inverse-kinematics-based planning. Each layer was validated before the next was built on top of it.

## Challenges & Solutions

- **Mechanical tolerance.** 3D-printed parts introduced backlash in the joints. Iterative reprinting and calibration of the joint assemblies tightened the tolerances until motion was repeatable.
- **Firmware reliability.** Noisy limit switches and abrupt stepper motion caused missed steps and jitter. Debouncing the switches and tuning the stepper acceleration curves in C++ produced smooth, dependable motion.
- **Coordinate frames.** Translating between world coordinates and joint space was the steepest learning curve of the project. Working through the kinematic transformations by hand, and verifying them against Arctos Studio's output, made the math concrete.

## Final Result

A working 6-DOF robotic arm that executes repeatable, programmable pick-and-place routines, planned graphically through inverse kinematics and executed through custom firmware. The total cost was a fraction of a comparable commercial arm.

## What I Learned

- Embedded and software skills are complementary. Getting the arm working required equal parts hardware debugging and software design, and neither could be skipped.
- Open-source robotics is a powerful learning tool. The Arctos community's documentation made it possible to focus effort on the engineering rather than reverse-engineering.
- This project deepened my interest in combining embedded systems with higher-level AI and ML for autonomous manipulation, a direction I continued in later robotics work.
